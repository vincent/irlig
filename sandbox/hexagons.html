 World map: Hex or Square
   
 Hodgman    Member since: 2/14/2007  From: Melbourne, Australia
 Posted - 2/5/2008 1:31:38 AM
I've been thinking about designing a simple empire-building/strategy game for a while, and I'm currently brainstorming ideas for the world-map.

The programmer in me wants to implement it as a grid of squares for simplicity, but the turn-based strategy lover in my wants to implement it as a grid of hexagons for... I'm not sure why for - familiarity perhaps?


So my question/discussion-point is: Does a grid of hexagons (or squares) have any special properties that are useful from a game-play-design point of view?


e.g. If I was making a board-game, then a hex-grid may be useful because it has the property of each cell having 6 neighbours, which means a D6 can be used for decisions (but I'm not making a board-game, so that property doesn't seem useful to me)...
Or if I was making a console game with an 8-way D-Pad input device, it would favour a square grid...
Surely there's some better reasons for choosing different grid styles?

 Iron Chef Carnage    Member since: 12/6/2002  From: Erie, PA, United States
 Posted - 2/5/2008 2:38:46 AM
Hexes have the advantage of not warping space as badly as squares do. In order to go diagonally, you have to make two moves, which makes angled travel a much greater distance than it actually would be in real space. Hexes have the same problem, but to a lesser degree. It also grants greater freedom to choose a complex path through the world.

Squares, as you noted, are easier to steer in, since 4- or 8-way controls are common, accepted and universally understood. 6-way is unintuitive. Also, your playing screen is likely to be a square or rectangle or parallelogram of some kind, making four-way orienteering even more natural.

Strengths, weaknesses, your best judgement.

Let's think out of the box, will you? We are not constrained by the Board...

So, what about... circles?

Theoretically, every point of the circle is at equal distance of the center, and you don't have to limit yourself to 4, 6 or eight directions.

In practice, you WILL want to limit yourself to 8 directions at most, because it is easier, right?

So how can we make that happen? I am not a specialist of path finding and intricate mapping systems, but maybe, if we layer TWO overlapping square-maps, we can probably get something that will resemble circles, provided no one looks too closely.

So, you'll be moving from intersection to intersection, and potentially from one map-layer to another map-layer. Provided each corner of a square on layer TWO is linked to four corners of the square it appears at the center of on layer ONE, you basically have a system that can be topped with a visual interface of circles without many people noticing something is strange. At least, if you're using isometric 3D, because in eagle-eye view, it will be rather plain, although not really dsiturbing, I think...

Just show circles, overlapping circles, and you can get yourself a pretty nifty innovative system...

Which means that hexes are in fact more intuitive than squares, not less.

The less apparent distortion along with the fact that you can only move into obviously connecting squares in a hex grid are the two qualities that beat 8-direction movement in a square grid (which is rather awkward) anytime.

Just because squares were implemented more often doesn't make hexes less intuitive by definition. Square grid is simply easier to implement.

The choice is still valid between a hex-based map and square-based one with 4-direction movement though. Then it really depends on the game.

I like your 'fake grid' idea, but I don't think it will work in all circumstances.
As it's mostly made up of edges/intersections, it works well for movement along it's connected pathways, but gets complicated when you start thinking about area. Particularly, because it doesn't actually tile properly, a point on the map could possibly be 'inside' two octagons/circles/etc at once.

You could get around this by having each cell represent an isolated piece of land (ignoring the fact that it would actually overlap it's neighbours), but in my game I need to render the contents of neighbouring cells at the same time in a continuous space - so overlapping spaces become a problem.


Quote:
Original post by polyfrag
Wouldn't hex grid be very hard to program, in terms of figuring out where the mouse clicks (on which hex it falls on)...

It's more complicated than squares, but a good game framework should be able to determine if a click is inside a convex-polygon (it's just a bunch of dot-products), so clicks aren't really a problem.

Quote:
And how would you represent it in memory? Well, I guess with a 2D array but with each second row shifted... But there's probably a solution I'm just not aware of...

Yeah, if you draw the bounding-boxes of each hex, it's basically a 2D array of horizontally-overlapping rectangles, with every 2nd column shifted vertically by half a cell.

Quote:
...and in terms of AI. For example, calculating the shortest path from A to B?

As long as you know the center position of each cell, you can still measure euclidean distance (as the crow flies) as usual between two points - this distance can still be used as a heuristic in A*.
Finding the neighbours of each cell isn't too hard either, e.g. assuming every 2nd column of cells is shifted "cell_height/2" upwards: 
void neighbours( int x, int y, vector<pair<int,int> >& out )
{
  out.push_back( make_pair(x,   y+1) );
  out.push_back( make_pair(x,   y-1) );
  out.push_back( make_pair(x+1, y  ) );
  out.push_back( make_pair(x-1, y  ) );
  int offs = ( x%2 ) ? 1 : -1;
  out.push_back( make_pair(x+1, y+offs) );
  out.push_back( make_pair(x-1, y+offs) );
}

 Posted - 2/7/2008 3:03:59 AM
> Does a grid of hexagons (or squares) have any special properties that are useful from a game-play-design point of view?

In my opinion, hexagons are superior if position and blocking maneuvers are valid strategies in your game. Also each adjacent cell has the same length shared edge.

It also makes distance calculations a bit more realistic than pure grid offsets (a + b) because a hexagonal grid acts more like circles of increasing radii than a square grid would.

Of course if you use pythagoras (sqrt(a^2 + b^2) for your distance calculations then the above becomes moot. Computers are good at that. Humans not so good in real time.

I think turn-based games are more suited to hexagonal grids than real-time ones are.

As for hexagonal graphical tiles, one interesting property is that the tiles do not have to be perfect hexagons i.e. 60 degrees at each angle. That is because, as Hodgman put it, a hexagonal grid is really just a square grid with every other row/column being offset. I was reading on the Wesnoth forums and they had a good argument that if you use graphical tiles, it is better to have the top and bottom of the hexagonal tile be flat as opposed to the left and right. That is because tiling isometric views are easier with this orientation.

One more thing to consider is that hexagonal grids would work better with mouse points and clicks for movement but not so good with keyboard movement.

Graphically? No. And that's a BIG no. I am not only way below par with graphics, but I am actually so bad it could end up showing absolutely anything, including something that wasn't intended in the first place. A geometrical descritpion might be way better.

Let's take four squares ABCD, DCEF, DFGH and DHIA. They all share sides with the neighbours.
Consider A' to be the geometrical center of ABCD, D' to be the center of DCEF, H' to be the geometrical center of DFGH and I' to be the center of ADHI.
I'A'D'H' is a square with EXACTLY identical dimensions to IADH, but moved for a squareroot of 2 in the ID direction.

Apply the same reasoning to ALL squares of you original grid.

What you now have is TWO overlapping square grids.

Now, consider M to be the intersection point of HD and I'H', N to be the intersection point of I'A' and AD, O to be the intersection point of DC and A'D', and P to be the intersection point of DF and H'D'

If D is at the center of your original square grid, then I', A', H' and D' are situated at a distance of a square root of 2 from D, whereas M,N,O and P are at a distance of 1/2 from D.

What you have, in practice, is a warped map, with intersecting diagonals, and you're not moving from square to square, but from intersection point to intersection point.

Provided you overlay a graphical interface showing a CIRCLE around each point, of a radius of a square root of 2 for the original grid, and with a radius of 1/2 for each point of the overlapping grid, you have the illusion that whatever move you intend to make is EXACTLY the same distance, since both circles always overlap at the same distance from the origin point.

I'll grant you it's not perfect, but it's probably easier than finding new rules for non-euclidian mathematics, in order to be able to draw a perfect octogonal grid...
